2. Age distribution analysis
Case mix & risk by age

Mammography screening spans a wide age range, and both cancer risk and reading behaviour change with age. The grouped bars show the percentage of each age band that falls into cancer, false positive, or normal categories, highlighting which groups dominate the dataset.

The rate curves then track how often cancer and false positives occur per age group, revealing that cancer risk typically rises with age while false positives often peak in the middle screening decades where breast density and recall thresholds interact.

Key insight

If the cancer rate steadily climbs across age bands while the false positive rate peaks in the mid‑life screening window, AI systems must be evaluated within age strata. Otherwise, performance could look good overall while masking poor detection in the youngest or oldest patients.

4. Energy settings analysis (RQ2)
kVp vs. labels

Tube energy (in keV) affects image noise, contrast, and penetration, and may be chosen differently for dense breasts, diagnostic work‑ups, or specific manufacturers. If cancers are systematically imaged at different energies than normals, models can pick up energy shortcuts instead of lesion cues.

The “box plot” approximation summarises minimum, mean, and maximum energy by case type, while the distribution chart shows how often each discrete energy value is used. Together they test whether energy settings co‑vary with cancer status enough to require explicit debiasing.

After loading the CSV, this note will highlight whether cancer exams cluster at particular keV levels compared with normal and false positive exams.

5. Evaluation metrics explainer
Sensitivity, specificity & AUROC

In breast screening, missing cancers is far more harmful than recalling an extra normal patient. This section lets you adjust a hypothetical model’s sensitivity and see how specificity, false positive rate, and false negative rate respond given the dataset’s class distribution.

At 0% sensitivity (a naive “always normal” model) accuracy equals the proportion of non‑cancer exams – potentially high – but every cancer is missed. This motivates using AUROC and reporting sensitivity at fixed specificity (for example 95%) rather than plain accuracy when publishing results on this dataset.

Why AUROC & sensitivity@spec?

Because cancer prevalence in screening cohorts is low (around 1–2% in NL‑like programmes), even a model that misses many cancers can achieve apparently strong accuracy. Using AUROC captures how well the model ranks cancers above normals across all thresholds.

In deployment, however, radiologists must pick one operating point. Reporting sensitivity at fixed specificity (for example 95% or 97.5%) makes the trade‑off explicit: how many extra recalls we pay per cancer saved. The slider above lets you explore these trade‑offs against your actual cohort mix.

6. Research questions & open problems
Study design & next steps

These cards crystallise three concrete research questions that arise from this metadata, each focusing on a specific source of bias or design choice that matters when building clinical mammography AI systems.

For each, the “why it matters” line explains the clinical or methodological risk, and the “next step” suggests analyses or extra data that would turn this dashboard exploration into a publishable study.

RQ1 · Equipment & false positives
Does anode material independently predict false positive rate beyond patient age?
Why it matters: If certain anode spectra systematically produce more recalls at the same age and case mix, AI models may learn machine‑specific artefacts and under‑perform when moved to different vendors or protocols.
Next step: Fit a multivariable logistic regression or hierarchical model with false positive vs. non‑false positive as outcome, and anode material, age band, and site as predictors, then test whether anode effects remain significant after adjustment.
RQ2 · Energy as confounder
Are energy settings a systematic confounder between cancer and normal cases that AI models should correct for?
Why it matters: If cancers are routinely imaged at slightly higher or lower keV than normals, networks can shortcut via exposure statistics rather than tumour morphology, hurting robustness when protocols change.
Next step: Compare energy distributions within age, breast, and view strata and, where confounding is present, train models with explicit energy conditioning or adversarial debiasing to test whether performance generalises across keV shifts.
RQ3 · Clinically acceptable sensitivity
What is the minimum sensitivity threshold a model must achieve to be clinically useful given this dataset's ~1.25% cancer prevalence?
Why it matters: At low prevalence, each additional missed cancer means many thousands of women scanned; setting a clinical floor on sensitivity anchors development in real patient impact rather than leaderboard gains.
Next step: Use the metrics slider above to explore how many cancers would be missed per 100k exams at different sensitivities, and collaborate with breast radiologists to agree a minimum operating point (for example ≥95% sensitivity at ≥95% specificity) to target in prospective validation.
