Key insight — different clinical pipelines
NLBS is a population screening dataset: asymptomatic Canadian women invited routinely, where only around 1.25% harbor cancer—a realistic screening prevalence. Mammo‑MX is a diagnostic dataset: Mexican women who presented with symptoms or concerns, where roughly 14.4% of exams are suspicious or malignant. An AI model trained purely on Mammo‑MX would be dangerously overconfident if deployed in a low‑prevalence screening program.

Nigerian women develop breast cancer significantly younger than Canadian or Mexican patients: 46% of Nigerian cases arise between ages 20–39. NLBS captures almost no women under 45, and Mammo‑MX still skews older than the Nigerian burden. An AI model trained on NLBS has effectively never seen the age profile that dominates Nigerian breast cancer, risking under‑detection in younger patients.

How Cancer Is Recorded Differs Critically

NLBS uses biopsy‑confirmed binary labels; Mammo‑MX uses radiologist BIRADS assessment.

NLBS case types
Normal screening exams, false positives, and biopsy‑confirmed cancers.
Binary label: cancer yes/no, biopsy confirmed; with a special subset of “hard‑to‑state negatives” that behave like false positives.
Mammo‑MX BIRADS grading
Radiologist BIRADS categories from incomplete (B0) to known malignant (B6).
Six‑level BIRADS scale is used globally and is richer than a binary label. However, B4 “suspicious” is not biopsy confirmed, and even some B5 exams may lack histology.
Label quality matters for AI
NLBS uses the gold standard: biopsy confirmation. Mammo‑MX uses BIRADS radiologist assessment—B4 and B5 cases may not all be confirmed cancers. For AI training, such unconfirmed labels introduce noise: NLBS, despite having fewer suspicious cases, is more trustworthy for training a cancer‑detection model than a large but label‑noisy diagnostic cohort.

Breast density, radiation dose, and X‑ray equipment configuration shape detectability—but NLBS omits key variables.

Breast density is one of the strongest predictors of mammography difficulty: dense breasts obscure tumors and are more common in younger, often non‑Western populations. Nigerian women on average have higher breast density than Canadian women, yet no African mammography dataset currently records density explicitly. Any AI screening tool for Nigeria must account for density and dose interactions, but this signal cannot be learned directly from NLBS.

Cancer subtype distribution
Triple‑negative (TNBC) vs ER+/PR+ vs other subtypes in Nigeria compared with a Western average.
Effiong et al. (2025) report 42.77% of immunohistochemically profiled Nigerian tumors are triple‑negative, far above Western averages. Left‑sided disease is slightly more common than right‑sided (46.86% vs 44.41%), and invasive ductal carcinoma dominates.
The Nigeria gap
Nigeria has one of the highest breast cancer mortality rates in Africa: 32,278 new cases and 16,332 deaths per year (mortality‑to‑incidence ratio 0.51). Around 80% of cases present at late stage, and 42.77% of tumors are triple‑negative—the most aggressive, mammographically challenging subtype, concentrated in younger women. Neither NLBS nor Mammo‑MX was designed to train an AI model for this reality, underscoring the need for Nigeria‑specific screening datasets and models.

Open Research Problems

Targeted questions linking NLBS, Mammo‑MX, and Nigerian gaps to concrete research directions in the VAC Lab.

Would an AI model trained on NLBS perform adequately on Nigerian patients given the age and subtype mismatch?

Why it matters: Nigerian women present roughly 15 years younger, with TNBC‑dominant tumors, than the NLBS screening population.

What data or method is needed: Cross‑dataset evaluation and domain adaptation/transfer learning from NLBS to a Nigerian cohort with known outcomes.

Connection to VAC Lab: Extends the NLBS dataset paper directly by testing the generalizability of its models beyond the Canadian source population.

Can breast density (available in Mammo‑MX) be estimated from NLBS DICOM metadata without the explicit label?

Why it matters: Density is the most important missing variable in NLBS for building models that transfer to denser‑breasted populations.

What data or method is needed: Train a density estimator on Mammo‑MX (pixels and metadata), then apply it to NLBS DICOM statistics as a pseudo‑label.

Connection to VAC Lab: Leverages the lab’s expertise in computational imaging and DICOM analysis to enrich NLBS without new annotations.

What minimum dataset size and age distribution would a Nigerian screening dataset need for clinically useful sensitivity?

Why it matters: Designing a Nigerian screening cohort requires justification of sample size and age targeting before costly data collection.

What data or method is needed: Power analysis using the NLBS class‑imbalance framework, adapted to Nigerian prevalence and subtype mix.

Connection to VAC Lab: Builds directly on the NLBS dataset design methodology while centering an under‑served population.

Is the false‑positive rate in NLBS associated with equipment variables independently of patient age?

Why it matters: If certain machine settings drive more false alarms, this bias is correctable both clinically and in AI training data.

What data or method is needed: Logistic regression on NLBS metadata: false‑positive outcome vs anode type and energy settings, adjusting for age.

Connection to VAC Lab: Immediately actionable using only the existing NLBS CSV and the lab’s statistical modeling expertise—no images required.
